How a Terminal
is Drawn

Pixel by pixel, from the byte stream of a pseudo-terminal to the glyph cache on a GPU — and what every developer should know about the surface they stare at all day.
4,210 words  ·  17 min read

Suppose you have an idea for a new way to render text. Perhaps you want shaped ligatures, a font that breathes with your typing, or simply a faster path from a keypress to a phosphor-warm pixel on screen. Whatever the motivation, you'll quickly discover that what you call "the terminal" is actually a stack of three or four programs in a trench coat, each pretending to be a teletype from 19691.

The terminal is a particularly good place to start a study of computation. It's familiar ground, and the abstractions it offers — read a byte, write a byte, move the cursor, change the color — have been remarkably stable for half a century. Almost every change since then has been a layer added around these primitives, not a replacement for them.

§2.1Principles of Drawing Glyphs

At the core of terminal rendering is a loop that turns bytes into rectangles of pixels. The bytes arrive over a pseudo-terminal device; they are parsed by a state machine that recognizes escape sequences; the parsed instructions update an in-memory grid of cells; and finally a renderer walks that grid and asks the GPU2 to draw a glyph at each cell.

Each of those four stages hides a small universe of decisions. The parser must handle malformed input from forty years of compatibility hacks. The grid must track not only what character lives in each cell, but its color, its weight, its underline, whether it is part of a wide CJK glyph, and whether the cursor currently sits on top of it.

1The DEC VT100, released in 1978, is still the lingua franca. Your iTerm2 negotiates as xterm-256color — itself a fiction wrapped around a fiction.
2Or the CPU, if you're on a remote tty. The fastest terminals today (Alacritty, Ghostty, Wezterm) all upload glyphs as textures and draw with a single fragment shader.
